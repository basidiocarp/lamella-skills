# Discover PESTEL Analysis

Use this skill to assess macro-environment forces around a product, market, or expansion decision. The output should identify the external shifts that matter most, explain why they matter, and translate them into strategic implications.

## When to Use

- Assessing a new market or geography
- Stress-testing a roadmap against macro shifts
- Preparing strategy or leadership reviews with external context
- Evaluating whether outside forces increase or reduce product risk

## Boundaries

- Use `agile-pm/discover-competitive-analysis` for competitor positioning and differentiation.
- Use `agile-pm/discover-company-profile` for a one-company strategic briefing.
- Do not use this as a substitute for customer research or product analytics.

## Workflow

1. **Define the scope**
   State the product, geography, time horizon, and decision the analysis is meant to support.

2. **Assess each PESTEL category**
   Review:
   - political
   - economic
   - social
   - technological
   - environmental
   - legal

3. **Tie every factor to product impact**
   For each factor, explain how it could affect demand, pricing, compliance, adoption, delivery, or strategic timing.

4. **Separate signal from noise**
   Focus on the few forces that could materially change the strategy. Do not turn the analysis into a generic macro news list.

5. **Turn the scan into action**
   End with the main opportunities, threats, open questions, and the strategic adjustments worth considering now.

Use `references/template.md` when the user needs a fill-in structure for the analysis.

## Output Expectations

Produce:
- the analysis scope
- the key PESTEL factors
- the likely product or market impact
- the top opportunities and threats
- the recommended next actions or watch items

## Quality Checklist

- [ ] Scope and time horizon are explicit
- [ ] Each factor is tied to a product or market implication
- [ ] The analysis highlights only the highest-leverage external forces
- [ ] Opportunities and threats are both covered
- [ ] The final takeaway is decision-useful rather than descriptive
